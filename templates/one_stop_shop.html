User Manual

Step-by-step guides for all 11 safety tests using the FSSAI Routine Safety Kit. Best used alongside the physical kit.

Module 1 — Water Quality

Is the water safe to use?

Equipment Needed

Water Test Strips Fresh water sample

Procedure

  1. Collect a fresh water sample from the source you use for cooking, washing, or food preparation.
  2. Dip the test strip into the sample for exactly 2 seconds, then remove and hold horizontally.
  3. Wait 30 – 60 seconds without shaking or blotting the strip.
  4. Compare the colour pads on the strip to the colour chart provided in the strip packaging.

What to Look For

✓ Safe
pH: 6.5 – 8.5

Water is neutral to slightly alkaline. Safe for all food use.

✓ Safe
Chlorine: 50 – 100 ppm

Adequate for vegetable wash water. Confirms sanitisation is effective.

⚠ Monitor
Hardness: above 200 mg/L

Very hard water causes scale buildup in kettles and equipment. Consider softening.

✕ Action Required
pH: below 6.5 or above 8.5

Acidic or highly alkaline water affects food safety and taste. Use filtered or bottled water and contact your water testing lab.

FSSAI Standard: Food Safety and Standards (Food Products Standards and Food Additives) Regulations, 2011 — Schedule IV: Drinking Water Standards.

Module 2 — Dairy Products

Is the milk pure — or is water or starch added?

Test 1 of 2

Detecting Water Adulteration in Milk

Equipment Needed

Lactometer Tall measuring cylinder Milk sample

Procedure

  1. Fill the measuring cylinder with the milk sample.
  2. Gently lower the lactometer into the milk and allow it to float freely without touching the sides.
  3. Wait for it to settle completely, then read the value at the surface of the milk (the meniscus level).
  4. Note: the standard reading is calibrated at 27°C. Adjust if significantly warmer or colder.

What to Look For

✓ Pure
Reading: 26 – 32

Full-fat cow milk reads 26–32; richer buffalo milk may read higher. Normal range.

✕ Adulterated
Reading: below 26

Low reading indicates water dilution. Do not use for food preparation or sale.

Test 2 of 2

Detecting Starch in Milk or Paneer

Equipment Needed

Glass Beaker Dropper Iodine Solution (1%) Milk or paneer sample

Procedure

  1. Add 2 – 3 ml of milk to the beaker. For paneer: dissolve a small piece in 5 ml of warm water.
  2. Using the dropper, add 2 – 3 drops of 1% iodine solution directly to the sample.
  3. Observe the colour change within 30 seconds.

What to Look For

✓ Pure
No colour change

Sample remains off-white or slightly amber (iodine colour). No starch present.

✕ Adulterated
Blue or blue-black colour

Starch is present — a common adulterant used to thicken milk or increase paneer yield. Do not sell.

FSSAI Standard: FSSR 2011 — Part A, Regulation 2.1: Milk and Milk Products. Maximum permissible adulterants: nil.

Module 3 — Grains

Is artificial colour added to rice or pulses?

Equipment Needed

Glass Plate Soak Lime (Calcium Hydroxide / Chuna) Rice or pulse sample

Procedure

  1. Take a teaspoon of rice or pulse on the glass plate and spread into a single layer.
  2. Sprinkle a small amount of soaked lime (chuna / calcium hydroxide paste) directly onto the grains.
  3. Observe any colour change on the grains within 1 – 2 minutes.

What to Look For

✓ Pure
No colour change

Grains retain their natural colour. No artificial colouring present.

✕ Adulterated
Red or pink colour appears

Turmeric or Metanil Yellow (artificial colour) has been added — a banned practice particularly found in sella (parboiled) rice. Do not sell.

FSSAI Standard: FSSR 2011 — Part A, Regulation 2.4: Cereals, Pulses and Products. Artificial colouring of grains is strictly prohibited.

Module 4 — Fruits & Vegetables

Are vegetables coated with artificial dye?

Equipment Needed

Paraffin Liquid Cotton Balls Test vegetables (chilli, bitter gourd, or sweet potato)

Procedure

  1. Soak a cotton ball thoroughly in paraffin liquid.
  2. Firmly rub the outer surface of the vegetable skin — focus on green skin (chilli, bitter gourd) or red skin (sweet potato).
  3. Observe the colour absorbed by the cotton ball.

What to Look For

✓ Natural
No colour on cotton ball

Natural vegetables do not transfer colour. Safe to use.

✕ Dye Detected
Bright green or red on cotton

Malachite Green or Rhodamine B detected — both are banned carcinogenic dyes. Do not purchase or sell.

FSSAI Standard: FSSR 2011 — Part A, Regulation 2.3. Use of Malachite Green and Rhodamine B is a criminal offence under the Food Safety and Standards Act, 2006.

Module 5 — Spices & Tea

Are iron filings present in spices or tea?

Equipment Needed

Neodymium Magnet Glass Plate Tea leaves or dry spice powder

Procedure

  1. Spread the tea leaves or spice powder in a thin, even layer on the glass plate.
  2. Slowly move the neodymium magnet through and just above the sample in a sweeping motion.
  3. Observe the magnet closely for any particles clinging to it.
  4. Lift the magnet away and tap gently — iron filings will remain stuck while natural particles fall off.

What to Look For

✓ Pure
Nothing clings to magnet

Natural spices and tea do not contain iron. No adulteration detected.

✕ Adulterated
Iron particles cling to magnet

Iron filings added to increase weight — a common weight fraud. Consumption of iron filings is a health hazard. Do not sell.

FSSAI Standard: FSSR 2011 — Part A, Regulation 2.9: Spices and Condiments. Iron filings are classified as a metallic contaminant and are strictly prohibited.

Module 6 — Cooking Oils

Is the cooking oil adulterated with solid fat or starch?

Test 1 of 2

Detecting Solid Fat (Vanaspati) in Cooking Oil

Equipment Needed

Glass Plate Refrigerator or bowl of ice water Cooking oil sample

Procedure

  1. Place a few drops of the cooking oil on the glass plate.
  2. Place the glass plate in a refrigerator for 2 hours, or hold it above a bowl of ice water for 20 minutes.
  3. Remove and observe the oil without disturbing it.

What to Look For

✓ Pure
Remains liquid or slightly hazy

Pure vegetable oils (sunflower, groundnut, soybean) stay liquid or become slightly viscous when cold. This is normal.

✕ Adulterated
Solidifies or turns grainy white

Vanaspati (hydrogenated vegetable fat) or solid fat has been added. Do not use or sell.

⚠ Note: Coconut oil naturally solidifies below 25°C. Do not apply this test to coconut oil — it will always produce a false positive.
Test 2 of 2

Detecting Starch in Flavoured or Coconut Oil

Equipment Needed

Glass Beaker Dropper Iodine Solution (1%) Oil sample and water

Procedure

  1. Add 1 ml of oil to 5 ml of water in the glass beaker. Stir vigorously for 10 seconds.
  2. Using the dropper, add 2 – 3 drops of 1% iodine solution to the mixture.
  3. Allow the oil and water to separate, then observe the colour in the water layer only.

What to Look For

✓ Pure
Water layer: pale amber

Water layer remains pale yellow or amber (the natural colour of diluted iodine). No starch present in the oil.

✕ Adulterated
Water layer: blue or blue-black

Starch or flour paste added to increase viscosity — particularly found in adulterated coconut and flavoured oils. Do not sell.

FSSAI Standard: FSSR 2011 — Part A, Regulation 2.2: Edible Fats and Oils. Addition of vanaspati to refined vegetable oils without labelling declaration is prohibited. Starch adulteration is prohibited under all circumstances.
